Project Introduction

Background

SCOUT is part of an intelligence platform, relied upon by analysts and fraud investigators to streamline task-based operations. As part of a broader UI overhaul, I designed a new dashboard experience to act as a control center—surfacing high-priority tasks, providing real-time status updates, and aligning team efforts at a glance.

Problem

Without a centralized dashboard, users had no easy way to monitor team performance or task progression—leading to inefficiencies, misalignment, and growing frustration. With no dedicated PM or formal roadmap, the design process had to be fast, focused, and tightly attuned to user needs.

The goal: To deliver a prototype dashboard that would empower users to track KPIs, oversee team workload, and regain clarity over daily operations. This meant understanding core user pain points, identifying essential features, and designing a system that was both intuitive and future-ready.

My Roles

I led the end-to-end design process—ideating, prototyping, and building an interactive model that balanced user requirements with business goals.

Solution

I created a modular, interactive dashboard featuring task metrics, team overviews, and key insights to help analysts stay informed and in control. Designed as a central command surface, the dashboard aimed to reduce cognitive load while making complex data immediately actionable.

Research

Dashboard Development

At the time of designing the dashboard, I didn’t have direct access to end users. Instead, I collaborated closely with stakeholders, internal subject-matter experts, and existing product behavior to understand core needs and daily workflows.

I ran informal discovery sessions to define the dashboard’s role: it needed to provide a clear snapshot of task statuses and enable faster decisions for live investigations.

The design approach was shaped by insights gathered from:

  • Established user workflows within the platform
  • Feedback provided during client demo sessions
  • Ongoing internal reviews with analysts and engineers

While this method surfaced actionable insights, the lack of structured user validation meant some usability issues emerged later. In hindsight, even lightweight walkthroughs or wireframe tests would have strengthened alignment with real-world behaviors earlier.

This phase highlighted a key learning I now apply: embedding early, low-effort validation loops to reduce assumptions and build confidence in layout decisions from day one.

1.1 - Understanding the Industry Landscape

To ensure the dashboard matched analyst expectations and broader UX patterns, I conducted a competitive review of intelligence and SaaS platforms. Common design themes included:

  • High-level metrics for quick insights: Summary stats offered fast performance reads and guided daily priorities.
  • Interactive data visualisations: Graphs, countdowns, and trend indicators helped analysts spot changes and act quickly.
  • Customisable layouts: Flexible views allowed users to tailor dashboards around role or workflow.
  • Responsive design standards: Mobile-ready layouts supported analysts working across varied devices and locations.

These patterns shaped both layout choices and prioritization of features. Combined with stakeholder input, they helped ensure the dashboard design balanced platform conventions with real user demands.

1.2 - Understanding User Expectations

Following the industry and competitor review, I turned my focus to internal knowledge and client-facing feedback. Since the system was already embedded in investigative workflows, I needed to uncover how a new dashboard could reduce friction and enhance day-to-day decision-making.

With no product manager in place, I conducted informal interviews with stakeholders and reviewed support tickets, onboarding notes, and user feedback channels. My goal was to uncover consistent patterns across analysts, team leads, and managers to identfy any unmet needs.

One theme came through loud and clear: users didn’t want more tools. They wanted clarity.

They needed a quick, actionable overview of their tasks, team activity, and performance signals, all in one place.

Key Findings

  • Performance Visibility: Teams lacked clarity on whether targets were being met—most insight came from ad hoc or manual reports.
  • Live Task Tracking: Real-time context on case progress was missing, making prioritization difficult.
  • Time Awareness: Missed meetings, unclear deadlines, and repeated effort stemmed from poor visibility into time-bound tasks.
  • Ownership & Accountability: Users were often unaware of task assignees, leading to confusion and duplicated work.

User Snapshot

To summarise expectations across roles, I captured key frustrations and desired outcomes in a role-based table:

Role Frustration Desired Outcome
Team Lead Lack of visbility into their teams KPIs Clear view of team performance
Analyst Missed meetings and status updates Task-level clarity and reminders
Manager No access to live perfomance reporting High-level stats and performance charts

These insights shaped the dashboard’s guiding principle: surface clarity without complexity. That meant designing around visibility, prioritization, and team alignment—without overloading users with functionality they didn’t ask for.

1.3 - Affinity Mapping

To organize early insights from stakeholder sessions and onboarding feedback, I created a lightweight affinity map. This allowed key frustrations and needs to emerge organically—without slowing the project down with formal personas or journey maps.

Rather than relying on assumptions, I clustered insights directly from real comments. This kept priorities focused and enabled fast, evidence-based design decisions.

Key Findings

  • Clarity & Complexity:
    Users consistently asked for cleaner layouts, stronger visual cues, and clearer iconography.
  • Live Visibility:
    Tracking tasks, viewing team activity, and staying ahead of meetings were top priorities.
  • Flexible Control
    Many requested customizable views and improved export options for operational flexibility.
  • Main Frustrations
    Pale colors, slow data refresh, hidden task details, and cluttered screens consistently blocked productivity.

These findings grounded the dashboard’s early direction, helping to prioritize clarity, speed, and practical utility.

Research Summary

  • Extracted insight from indirect sources: Stakeholder interviews and onboarding feedback revealed real user needs despite limited access.
  • Synthesis stayed lean and actionable: Affinity mapping replaced formal artifacts, enabling fast, focused design under time pressure.
  • Adapted to organisational constraints: With no PM or researchers, I leaned into subject-matter expert reviews and scoped future validation loops.
  • Embedded early testing as a learning phase: This phase directly influenced my approach to future projects, placing emphasis on low-effort testing earlier in the design cycle.
  • Focused on clarity over features: Users didn’t want more—they wanted simplicity, visibility, and alignment. That insight shaped the dashboard’s functional scope.

Design Strategy

With a clear understanding of user needs, I defined a lean strategy to translate insight into meaningful design outcomes. The goal wasn’t to overload users with data, it was to create a role-aware experience that respected context, pace, and mental load.

Key strategic decisions included:

  • A modular, multi-row dashboard layout: This structure aligned with stakeholder input, separating high-level stats, task progress, team metrics, and time-sensitive actions into clear zones—all within a single, glanceable interface.
  • A focused prioritisation of core features: I concentrated on task tracking, team performance, and meeting reminders, ensuring the dashboard provided immediate value without overwhelming users with unnecessary functionality.
    Every design decision had to pass two questions: → Does this unlock faster decision-making? → Can it reduce manual effort? These kept the interface purposeful and user-first.
  • Micro-interactions over navigation shifts: Instead of complex navigation flows, I designed interactive elements that allowed users to drill down into task details or team stats without losing context. This kept the focus on immediate actions rather than navigating away from the dashboard.

This strategic baseline shaped both the low-fidelity prototypes and the final high-fidelity design—anchoring them in clarity, familiarity, and practical value.

1.2 - Data Visualisation & Interface Evolution

As the dashboard matured, specific components—like the Task Focus table and performance graph—underwent strategic iterations to improve clarity, scannability, and user actionability. These refinements were not limited to surface polish; they shaped the system’s broader visual language and established key patterns that informed the design of modal tables and secondary data charts.

The changes in this section laid the foundation for consistency and flexibility across the product. The aim was not to replicate the dashboard elements identically in every context, but to adapt them to local needs while keeping the overall experience intuitive and efficient.

Task Focus Table

This table began as a clean but inert record of tasks—readable, but detached from user goals around prioritisation and progress tracking. User expectations quickly revealed the need for a more responsive, action-ready interface. Iterative changes were guided by principles of scannability, hierarchy, and cognitive load reduction—turning the table into a decision-friendly surface. Small shifts in alignment, iconography, and visual encoding combined to create a tool that supports momentum, not just monitoring.

Design Improvements

  • Reordered Rows by Due Date: Brought time-sensitive tasks to the top, allowing users to triage more effectively and honouring natural scanning rhythms.
  • Introduced a Status Column with Icon + Label: Used recognisable icons with simple descriptors (e.g. checkmark, spinner) to make task progression immediately visible—supporting recognition and system status clarity.
  • Added Contextual Action Menu: Integrated per-row action triggers so users could interact with tasks directly—boosting workflow efficiency and reducing step count.
  • Enhanced Priority Column with Icon + Label: Introduced a traffic light icon alongside existing text (e.g. red icon next to “High”), using redundant coding to ensure urgency was visually and semantically conveyed. This improves accessibility and recognition.
  • Improved Column Alignment: Adopted left/right anchoring for better rhythm: task names left-aligned for quick skimming; dates and IDs right-aligned for numeric comparison.
  • Removed Vertical Borders: Cleared excess dividing lines to reduce clutter and improve table legibility without sacrificing structural clarity.
  • Applied Accessible Colour Contrast: Ensured all icon colours and status indicators met WCAG guidelines, improving usability for low-vision users and maintaining clarity in different viewing conditions.

1.2 - Interaction Design & Accessibility

Interactive behaviours were designed to feel intuitive and purposeful—providing visual feedback through motion, tone shifts, and spatial changes. From hover cues to pressed states, each component signals intent while reinforcing flow. Accessibility remained central as all elements are WCAG compliant to ensure inclusive usability across input types and visual abilities.

High level Card

Highights

  • Three-State Model: Clear default, hover, and pressed states provide intuitive affordance and reduce ambiguity.
  • Refined Hover Feedback: Background intensifies and elevation flattens, offering subtle interaction cues without visual noise.
  • Pressed State Response: A deeper background tone reinforces tap or click intent with minimal delay.
  • Navigational Entry Point: Cards link to deeper modal views, supporting progressive disclosure and exploratory flows.
  • Touch-Friendly Design: At 280×200px, the card exceeds accessibility touch targets across devices.
  • Accessible Contrast: Text remains WCAG AAA compliant across all tones; overlays use low-opacity values to aid legibility.
  • Responsive Motion: Animations are deliberate and concise—timed under 400ms to respect user attention and motion preferences.

Button States

The button states were designed to provide clear visual feedback and enhance user interaction. Each state was carefully crafted to ensure that users could easily understand the button's functionality and responsiveness.

Primary Button

Secondary Button

Highlights

  • Three-State System: Both Primary and Secondary buttons include clear Default, Hover, and Pressed states. This reinforces user confidence by providing consistent feedback across interaction points.
  • Accessible Contrast: All states meet or exceed WCAG 2.1 contrast guidelines. This ensures legibility for users with visual impairments, supporting both dark-on-light and light-on-dark combinations depending on button type.
  • Primary Button Emphasis: A bold, coloured background with white text clearly communicates high-priority actions. Subtle tonal shifts on hover and press enhance interactivity without straining visual comfort.
  • Secondary Button Subtlety: A lighter, inverse treatment with a white base and coloured text supports utility actions without competing for attention. Hover and pressed states use background fills and tonal changes to indicate interactivity.
  • Consistent Feedback Behaviour: Both button types follow the same interaction model, motion logic, and transition speed. This consistency supports predictability and reduces cognitive load, especially in interfaces with both button types present.
  • Touch-Optimised Sizing: Button dimensions and internal padding comfortably exceed minimum tap target standards (44×44px), enhancing usability across touch, pointer, and hybrid input modes.

Scaling Interaction Patterns

Following the same principles used in cards and buttons, data visualisations were designed with clarity, accessibility, and thoughtful feedback in mind. The donut chart introduced hover-based labels that revealed insights when relevant, keeping the interface clear and focused. This interaction model was then consistently applied across all charts to support easy exploration and reduce cognitive effort.

Highlights

  • Progressive Disclosure: Values are revealed only on hover to minimise cognitive load and preserve initial clarity.
  • Spatial Feedback: Label bubbles appear contextually near each chart segment, aiding association and reducing interpretation time.
  • Consistent Interaction Pattern: This reveal-on-hover model was applied across all data visualisations—donut, line, and bar charts—creating predictable, low-effort information access.
  • Reduced Visual Noise: Hiding values by default prevents clutter in dense dashboards while supporting accessibility when surfaced.
  • Scalable Design Logic: The pattern adapts seamlessly across both dashboard and modal environments, ensuring coherence across layouts and chart types.

Data Visualisation


Data visualisation is more than just presenting numbers; it’s about transforming complex datasets into meaningful insights that users can quickly understand and act upon. In this case study, the focus was on creating a dashboard that not only displayed data but also facilitated intuitive exploration and interpretation.


Achieving that required more than clean visuals it demanded purposeful design. Visual formats were selected for their fit to the task, information was structured for legibility, and established principles guided each decision. From layout to visual encoding, every element aimed to reduce friction and increase trust, helping users interpret performance at speed with confidence.

1.1 a - Tabular Structure & Readability

Before visualisations could guide exploration, the table needed to communicate structure and intent. Raw metrics were grouped meaningfully, aligned for legibility, and spaced to support fast scanning.


UX principles such as Hick’s Law, Fitts’ Law, and proximity informed layout and interaction decisions—ensuring each element supported quick comparison without visual noise.

Table Design Overview

The layout followed the user’s task flow: high-level identifiers on the left, granular details on the right. Text was left-aligned, numbers right-aligned, and spacing reinforced grouping. This structure laid the foundation for further refinements.

Table Headers

Original field names were rewritten for clarity and task relevance. Labels were shortened, restructured, and aligned to support scanning and reduce friction.


Visual Indicators

Priority was conveyed using the traffic light system consisting of red, amber, and green paired with text labels for accessibility. Progress states used icons with short labels to reinforce meaning without relying on color alone. All indicators met WCAG contrast standards and were spaced for clarity and alignment.


Contextual Menu

To reduce clutter, secondary actions were moved into a contextual menu revealed on hover or tap. Positioned consistently at the row’s end, it preserved spatial memory and kept the interface clean. Interaction states followed Material Design guidance to ensure clarity and accessibility.


Design Comparison

The final table design reflects a series of deliberate refinements across structure, hierarchy, and interaction. Compared to the low-fidelity layout, the high-fidelity version improves scanability, reduces cognitive load, and introduces visual cues that support both speed and comprehension.
